    IN ACCORDANCE TO THE BHARITIYA NYAYA SANHITA (Laws) Section 63. A man is said to commit “rape” if he— (a) penetrates his penis, to any extent, into the vagina, mouth, urethra or anus of a woman or makes her to do so with him or any other person; or (b) inserts, to any extent, any object or a part of the body, not being the penis, into the vagina, the urethra or anus of a woman or makes her to do so with him or any other person; or (c) manipulates any part of the body of a woman so as to cause penetration into the vagina, urethra, anus or any part of body of such woman or makes her to do so with him or any other person; or (d) applies his mouth to the vagina, anus, urethra of a woman or makes her to do so with him or any other person, under the circumstances falling under any of the following seven descriptions:— (i) against her will; (ii) without her consent; (iii) with her consent, when her consent has been obtained by putting her or any person in whom she is interested, in fear of death or of hurt; (iv) with her consent, when the man knows that he is not her husband and that her consent is given because she believes that he is another man to whom she is or believes herself to be lawfully married; (v) with her consent when, at the time of giving such consent, by reason of unsoundness of mind or intoxication or the administration by him personally or through another of any stupefying or unwholesome substance, she is unable to understand the nature and consequences of that to which she gives consent; (vi) with or without her consent, when she is under eighteen years of age; (vii) when she is unable to communicate consent. Explanation 1.—For the purposes of this section, “vagina” shall also include labia majora. Explanation 2.—Consent means an unequivocal voluntary agreement when the woman by words, gestures or any form of verbal or non-verbal communication, communicates willingness to participate in the specific sexual act: Provided that a woman who does not physically resist to the act of penetration shall not by the reason only of that fact, be regarded as consenting to the sexual activity. Exception 1.––A medical procedure or intervention shall not constitute rape. Exception 2.––Sexual intercourse or sexual acts by a man with his own wife, the wife not being under eighteen years of age, is not rape. Punishment for rape. Section 64. (1) Whoever, except in the cases provided for in sub-section (2), commits rape, shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment of either description for a term which shall not be less than ten years, but which may extend to imprisonment for life, and shall also be liable to fine. (2) Whoever, — (a) being a police officer, commits rape, —. (i) within the limits of the police station to which such police officer is appointed; or (ii) in the premises of any station house; or (iii) on a woman in such police officer’s custody or in the custody of a police officer subordinate to such police officer; or (b) being a public servant, commits rape on a woman in such public servant’s custody or in the custody of a public servant subordinate to such public servant; or (c) being a member of the armed forces deployed in an area by the Central Government or a State Government commits rape in such area; or (d) being on the management or on the staff of a jail, remand home or other place of custody established by or under any law for the time being in force or of a women’s or children’s institution, commits rape on any inmate of such jail, remand home, place or institution; or (e) being on the management or on the staff of a hospital, commits rape on a woman in that hospital; or (f) being a relative, guardian or teacher of, or a person in a position of trust or authority towards the woman, commits rape on such woman; or (g) commits rape during communal or sectarian violence; or (h) commits rape on a woman knowing her to be pregnant; or (i) commits rape, on a woman incapable of giving consent; or (j) being in a position of control or dominance over a woman, commits rape on such woman; or (k) commits rape on a woman suffering from mental or physical disability; or (l) while committing rape causes grievous bodily harm or maims or disfigures or endangers the life of a woman; or (m) commits rape repeatedly on the same woman, shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment for a term which shall not be less than ten years, but which may extend to imprisonment for life, which shall mean imprisonment for the remainder of that person’s natural life, and shall also be liable to fine. Explanation.—For the purposes of this sub-section,— (a) “armed forces” means the naval, army and air forces and includes any member of the Armed Forces constituted under any law for the time being in force, including the paramilitary forces and any auxiliary forces that are under the control of the Central Government or the State Government; (b) “hospital” means the precincts of the hospital and includes the precincts of any institution for the reception and treatment of persons during convalescence or of persons requiring medical attention or rehabilitation; (c) “police officer” shall have the same meaning as assigned to the expression “police” under the Police Act, 1861; (d) “women’s or children’s institution” means an institution, whether called an orphanage or a home for neglected women or children or a widow’s home or an institution called by any other name, which is established and maintained for the reception and care of women or children. (1) Whoever, commits rape on a woman under sixteen years of age shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment for a term which shall not be less than twenty years, but which may extend to imprisonment for life, which shall mean imprisonment for the remainder of that person’s natural life, and shall also be liable to fine: Provided that such fine shall be just and reasonable to meet the medical expenses and rehabilitation of the victim: Provided further that any fine imposed under this sub-section shall be paid to the victim. (2) Whoever, commits rape on a woman under twelve years of age shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment for a term which shall not be less than twenty years, but which may extend to imprisonment for life, which shall mean imprisonment for the remainder of that person’s natural life, and with fine or with death: Provided that such fine shall be just and reasonable to meet the medical expenses and rehabilitation of the victim: Provided further that any fine imposed under this sub-section shall be paid to the victim. Section 72. Disclosure of identity of victim of certain offences, etc. (1) Whoever prints or publishes the name or any matter which may make known the identity of any person against whom an offence under section 64 or section 65 or section 66 or section 67 or section 68 or section 69 or section 70 or section 71 is alleged or found to have been committed (hereafter in this section referred to as the victim) shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to two years and shall also be liable to fine. (2) Nothing in sub-section (1) extends to any printing or publication of the name or any matter which may make known the identity of the victim if such printing or publication is— (a) by or under the order in writing of the officer-in-charge of the police station or the police officer making the investigation into such offence acting in good faith for the purposes of such investigation; or (b) by, or with the authorisation in writing of, the victim; or (c) where the victim is dead or a child or of unsound mind, by, or with the authorisation in writing of, the next of kin of the victim: Provided that no such authorisation shall be given by the next of kin to anybody other than the chairman or the secretary, by whatever name called, of any recognised welfare institution or organisation. Explanation.—For the purposes of this sub-section, “recognised welfare institution or organisation” means a social welfare institution or organisation recognised in this behalf by the Central Government or the State Government. Section 73. Printing or publishing any matter relating to Court proceedings without permission. Central Victim Compensation Fund (CVCF), 2015 The Central Victim Compensation Fund (CVCF) was introduced by the Ministry of Home Affairs on 14 October 2015, under the framework of Section 357A of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973. It was established with an initial corpus of ₹200 crore from the Nirbhaya Fund to provide financial support to victims of serious crimes, including rape, sexual assault, acid attacks, child sexual offences, and human trafficking. The CVCF was created to supplement and standardise State and Union Territory Victim Compensation Schemes by ensuring uniform minimum compensation and reducing disparities across regions. Funds are disbursed to States/UTs that have notified their own schemes under Section 357A CrPC. The scheme’s guidelines, issued on 14 October 2015 and revised in July 2016, specify eligibility, procedures, and minimum compensation amounts for victims. It is a non-lapsable corpus under the Ministry of Finance created to support initiatives aimed at enhancing the safety, security, and dignity of women. The fund finances schemes related to: • Rehabilitation and compensation of rape and sexual assault survivors. • Setting up emergency response systems, safe transport, and women’s helplines. • Strengthening mechanisms for prevention and response to gender-based violence.     PROGRAMMES & SCHEMES 1. One Stop Centres (OSC) The One Stop Centre (OSC) scheme was launched by the Ministry of Women and Child Development (MWCD) in 2015 under the Nirbhaya Fund. It is designed to provide integrated support and assistance to women affected by violence, whether that violence occurs in public or private spaces. Each centre acts as a safe space where women in distress can receive medical help, legal aid, police assistance, counselling, and temporary shelter—all under one roof. The main goal is to ensure that survivors of violence are not left to navigate the system alone but instead receive compassionate and coordinated care. These centres are usually located within or near district hospitals to make emergency services easily accessible. They function around the clock and are equipped to respond to both immediate crises and ongoing support needs. The OSC model follows a survivor-centric approach that prioritises a woman’s safety, dignity, and privacy. Every survivor is treated with empathy and respect, and no woman is denied help because she lacks identification or hesitates to share personal details. Confidentiality is always maintained unless disclosure is required by law. Each centre works in close coordination with other mechanisms such as the Women Helpline (181), Anti-Human Trafficking Units, Legal Services Authorities, and Fast Track Courts. This ensures that survivors have access many services like filing a case, receiving medical care and getting long-term counselling and rehabilitation. The centres also provide temporary accommodation for women and their children for up to five days or more, according to circumstances. This safe space allows survivors time to recover emotionally and make informed decisions about their next steps. The OSCs are managed through a three-tier system. At the national level, the MWCD oversees policy and funding. At the state level, the Department of Women and Child Development is responsible for implementation. At the district level, the centres are supervised by the District Magistrate and a Management Committee made up of police, legal, health, and civil society representatives. Each centre is run primarily by women professionals, including a Centre Administrator, case workers, counsellors, legal and medical officers, IT staff, and security personnel. These staff members receive specialised training from SPNIWCD in areas such as survivor care, trauma counselling, and crisis management. The Ministry also conducts regular monitoring and evaluations of each centre. Survivor feedback, field inspections, and data reporting through the Mission Shakti Dashboard help ensure that services remain accountable, effective, and sensitive. Today, more than 700 One Stop Centres operate across India, offering women a lifeline of support and a path to recovery. The OSC scheme is a key part of India’s broader mission to create a nation where every woman can live with safety, dignity, and equal opportunity — truly reflecting the spirit of “Nari Shakti” and advancing Sustainable Development Goal 5: Gender Equality and Women’s Empowerment. 2. Swadhar Greh Scheme The Swadhar Greh Scheme was introduced by the Government of India in 2015 under the Ministry of Women and Child Development (MWCD). It aims to provide integrated shelter and support services to women in distress who have been left without social or economic security. • The scheme seeks to ensure dignity, care, and rehabilitation for women facing difficult circumstances such as domestic violence, human trafficking, sexual assault, desertion, natural disasters, or social exclusion. • It merges two earlier programmes — Swadhar (2001) and Short Stay Homes — to create a unified framework for women’s welfare and recovery. • Each Swadhar Greh serves as a safe and supportive environment where women can live temporarily while rebuilding their lives. • Facilities include shelter, food, clothing, counselling, medical care, legal aid, and vocational training to enable economic independence and reintegration into society. • The scheme priorities rehabilitation and empowerment, encouraging women to regain self-confidence and self-reliance rather than long-term institutionalisation. • It is implemented through State Governments, Union Territories, and recognised NGOs, with financial assistance provided by the Central Government. • Preference is given to women victims of violence, trafficked women rescued from exploitative conditions, widows, and women released from prisons who lack family support. • Each home can accommodate up to 30 women, with flexibility based on regional needs. • The scheme also allows children accompanying the women (usually up to 18 years of age) to stay with them and access basic education and care. 3. Compensation Scheme for Women Victims/ Survivors of Sexual Assault/ Other Crimes (2018, NLSA) The scheme was formulated by NALSA (National Legal Services Authority) in consultation with the Ministry of Women and Child Development under Section 357A of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973. It aims to provide financial relief and rehabilitation to women who are victims of rape, sexual assault, acid attacks, or other crimes leading to physical or mental suffering. States and Union Territories are required to adopt this as a model scheme or modify their existing victim compensation schemes to ensure uniformity across India. Compensation is to be provided irrespective of the criminal proceedings — meaning even if the accused is not identified or convicted, victims remain eligible. The scheme ensures interim compensation is provided to victims within 60 days of application to meet immediate needs such as medical treatment, shelter, and rehabilitation. Minimum and maximum compensation amounts are prescribed depending on the nature of the offence: Rape and unnatural sexual assault: ₹4–7 lakh Acid attack (disfigurement beyond recognition): up to ₹8 lakh Loss of foetus or infertility due to assault: up to ₹2 lakh Physical abuse of minor girls: ₹2–5 lakh The District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) or State Legal Services Authority (SLSA) is responsible for processing and disbursing compensation applications. Victims may also be referred to medical, psychological, and legal counselling services through the legal aid network.
