    IN ACCORDANCE TO THE BHARITIYA NYAYA SANHITA (Laws) Hiring, employing or engaging a child to commit an offence. Section 95. Whoever hires, employs or engages any child to commit an offence shall be punished with imprisonment of either description which shall not be less than three years but which may extend to ten years, and with fine; and if the offence be committed shall also be punished with the punishment provided for that offence as if the offence has been committed by such person himself. Explanation.—Hiring, employing, engaging or using a child for sexual exploitation or pornography is covered within the meaning of this section. Organised crime. Section 111. (1) Any continuing unlawful activity including kidnapping, robbery, vehicle theft, extortion, land grabbing, contract killing, economic offence, cyber-crimes, trafficking of persons, drugs, weapons or illicit goods or services, human trafficking for prostitution or ransom, by any person or a group of persons acting in concert, singly or jointly, either as a member of an organized crime syndicate or on behalf of such syndicate, by use of violence, threat of violence, intimidation, coercion, or by any other unlawful means to obtain direct or indirect material benefit including a financial benefit, shall constitute organised crime. Kidnapping or abducting in order to murder or for ransom, etc. Section 140. (4) Whoever kidnaps or abducts any person in order that such person may be subjected, or may be so disposed of as to be put in danger of being subjected to grievous hurt, or slavery, or to the unnatural lust of any person, or knowing it to be likely that such person will be so subjected or disposed of, shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to ten years, and shall also be liable to fine. Trafficking of person. Section 143. (1)Whoever, for the purpose of exploitation recruits, transports, harbours, transfers, or receives a person or persons, by— (a) using threats; or (b) using force, or any other form of coercion; or (c) by abduction; or (d) by practising fraud, or deception; or (e) by abuse of power; or (f) by inducement, including the giving or receiving of payments or benefits, in order to achieve the consent of any person having control over the person recruited, transported, harboured, transferred or received, commits the offence of trafficking. Explanation 1.—The expression “exploitation” shall include any act of physical exploitation or any form of sexual exploitation, slavery or practices similar to slavery, servitude, beggary or forced removal of organs. Explanation 2.—the consent of the victim is immaterial in determination of the offence of trafficking. (2) Whoever commits the offence of trafficking shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment for a term which shall not be less than seven years, but which may extend to ten years, and shall also be liable to fine. (3) Where the offence involves the trafficking of more than one person, it shall be punishable with rigorous imprisonment for a term which shall not be less than ten years but which may extend to imprisonment for life, and shall also be liable to fine. (7) When a public servant or a police officer is involved in the trafficking of any person then, such public servant or police officer shall be punished with imprisonment for life, which shall mean imprisonment for the remainder of that person’s natural life, and shall also be liable to fine. Habitual dealing in slaves. Section 145. Whoever habitually imports, exports, removes, buys, sells, traffics or deals in slaves, shall be punished with imprisonment for life, or with imprisonment of either description for a term not exceeding ten years, and shall also be liable to fine. Unlawful compulsory labour. Section 146. Whoever unlawfully compels any person to labour against the will of that person, shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to one year, or with fine, or with both. SUB SECTION (Human Trafficking) Forced Prostitution Procuration of child. Section 96. Whoever, by any means whatsoever, induces any child to go from any place or to do any act with intent that such child may be, or knowing that it is likely that such child will be, forced or seduced to illicit intercourse with another person shall be punishable with imprisonment which may extend to ten years, and shall also be liable to fine. Selling child for purposes of prostitution, etc. Section 98. Whoever sells, lets to hire, or otherwise disposes of any child with intent that such child shall at any age be employed or used for the purpose of prostitution or illicit intercourse with any person or for any unlawful and immoral purpose, or knowing it to be likely that such child will at any age be employed or used for any such purpose, shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to ten years, and shall also be liable to fine. Explanation 1.—When a female under the age of eighteen years is sold, let for hire, or otherwise disposed of to a prostitute or to any person who keeps or manages a brothel, the person so disposing of such female shall, until the contrary is proved, be presumed to have disposed of her with the intent that she shall be used for the purpose of prostitution Buying child for the purpose of prostitution, etc. Section 99. Exploitation of a trafficked person. Section 144. (1) Whoever, knowingly or having reason to believe that a child has been trafficked, engages such child for sexual exploitation in any manner, shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment for a term which shall not be less than five years, but which may extend to ten years, and shall also be liable to fine. The Suppression of Immoral Traffic in Women and Girls Act, 1956 The Suppression of Immoral Traffic in Women and Girls Act, 1956 was enacted to prevent and control the trafficking and sexual exploitation of women and girls. It implements the 1950 International Convention signed at New York and came into force on 1 May 1958. The Act extends to the whole of India. The Act defines key terms such as: • Brothel: Any place used for prostitution for the gain of another person. • Prostitute: A woman who offers her body for promiscuous sexual intercourse for hire. • Prostitution: The act of offering sexual intercourse for hire. • Girl: Female below 21 years of age. • Woman: Female above 21 years of age. • Protective home: An institution for the rescue, protection, and rehabilitation of women and girls engaged or forced into prostitution. Major Provisions and Punishments (Women-Centered Focus): 1. Keeping or managing a brothel (Section 3): Anyone involved in running or allowing premises to be used as a brothel is punishable with 1–3 years’ imprisonment and fine up to ₹2000; for subsequent offences, 2–5 years. 2. Living on the earnings of prostitution (Section 4): Persons exploiting a woman’s income from prostitution face up to 2 years’ imprisonment or fine up to ₹1000, or both. 3. Procuring or inducing women or girls for prostitution (Section 5): Those who procure, induce, or take women or girls for prostitution face 1–2 years’ imprisonment and fine up to ₹2000; 2–5 years for repeat offences. 4. Detaining women or girls for prostitution (Section 6): Detaining any woman or girl, even with her consent, for prostitution is punishable with 1–2 years’ imprisonment and fine up to ₹2000; 2–5 years for subsequent offences. 5. Prostitution near public places (Section 7): Prostitution within 200 yards of temples, schools, hospitals, or similar places is prohibited and punishable with up to 3 months’ imprisonment. 6. Soliciting or seducing for prostitution (Section 8): Women found soliciting in public places may face up to 6 months’ imprisonment or ₹500 fine; for repeat offences, up to 1 year and ₹500 fine. 7. Seducing women or girls in custody (Section 9): Any person who seduces or abets the seduction of a woman or girl under their care for prostitution faces 1–3 years’ imprisonment and ₹1000 fine; up to 5 years for repeat offences. Protection and Rehabilitation Measures: • Courts can release first-time offenders on probation or admonition, depending on circumstances. • Women or girls convicted of prostitution-related offences may be detained in protective homes for 2–5 years instead of imprisonment, where they receive moral, educational, and vocational support. Rescue and Custody (Sections 15–17): • Police and magistrates have powers to rescue women and girls from brothels or places of exploitation, even without a warrant in urgent situations. • Rescued women are to be produced before a magistrate and placed in safe custody or protective homes, ensuring their protection from further exploitation. • The Act prohibits keeping rescued women in custody run by persons of a different religion to ensure comfort and trust. Protective Homes (Section 21): • The State Government may establish or license protective homes for the care, training, and rehabilitation of women and girls. • Only licensed authorities can maintain such homes. Unlicensed operation leads to a fine up to ₹1000 (first offence) and imprisonment up to one year (subsequent offence). • Wherever possible, management of these homes must be entrusted to women. This Act (SITA, 1956) was later amended and renamed as the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, 1956 (ITPA) in 1986, which remains in force today. Ujjwala Scheme Ujjawala Scheme (Ministry of Women and Child Development, Government of India, launched in 2007) The Ujjawala Scheme is a comprehensive initiative by the Ministry of Women and Child Development (MWCD) aimed at preventing trafficking of women and children for commercial sexual exploitation and ensuring their rescue, rehabilitation, and reintegration into society. • The scheme adopts a five-component approach: • Prevention – through community vigilance, awareness generation, and social mobilisation in vulnerable areas to reduce trafficking risks. • Rescue – by working with law enforcement agencies to identify and rescue victims from exploitative conditions. • Rehabilitation – by providing safe housing, medical care, counselling, legal aid, and vocational training through shelter homes and protective services. • Reintegration – through family reunification or placement in alternative care systems, ensuring survivors regain dignity and stability. • Repatriation – of cross-border victims in coordination with appropriate authorities. • The scheme is centrally sponsored, with 100% funding from the Government of India for project setup costs and 75:25 (Centre:State) cost sharing for recurring expenses (90:10 for North-Eastern States). • Implementation is carried out through State Governments, Union Territories, and recognised NGOs that operate Ujjawala Homes and rehabilitation centres under MWCD supervision. • Beneficiaries receive comprehensive support, including medical and psychological care, legal assistance, education, skill training, and employment linkages to facilitate independent living. • The scheme operates in close coordination with other MWCD initiatives such as Swadhar Greh, One Stop Centres, and the 181 Women Helpline to provide integrated services for women in distress. • Periodic monitoring and evaluation is conducted through State Governments and independent agencies to ensure transparency, effectiveness, and survivor welfare. • The Ujjawala Scheme aligns with India’s commitments under international conventions like the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW) and the UN Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons, Especially Women and Children. 2. Mahila Police Volunteers (MPV) Scheme Launched jointly by the Ministry of Women and Child Development (MWCD) and the Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA), the MPV Scheme aims to link police forces with local communities via trained female volunteers who assist women in distress and help promote gender-sensitive policing. The initiative began on a pilot basis in two districts in Haryana (Karnal and Mahendragarh) during the 2016-17 fiscal year under the “Nirbhaya Fund”. Key features and responsibilities of the scheme include: • Selecting socially aware women volunteers (typically age 21 or above, with at least higher secondary education) from local areas to serve as interfaces between the community and the police. • Mobilising community participation, generating awareness about services available to women and children, and reporting incidents of violence, trafficking, child marriage, dowry harassment or missing children to the police. • Forming local-level women-and-child protection groups such as “Mahila and Shishu Rakshak Dals” (MASRDs) and integrating existing community groups into those structures. • Operating as a central sector scheme funded under the Nirbhaya Fund, with implementation by State/UT Governments in collaboration with MWCD/MHA. Monitoring details: By mid-2019, the scheme had been approved for 13 States/UTs and operationalised in 7 of them (Andaman & Nicobar Islands, Andhra Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Gujarat, Haryana, Mizoram, Tripura) with over 9,500 volunteers appointed.

    Connects women directly to police, hospitals, One Stop Centres, and legal authorities for safety, support, and protection in emergencies or personal crises.

    181 – Women Helpline:

    A 24×7 helpline offering emergency response, counselling, legal guidance, and information on government welfare schemes for women.

    A single, all-India emergency number for police, fire, and ambulance services under the Nirbhaya Fund scheme, ensuring rapid response across all states and UTs.
